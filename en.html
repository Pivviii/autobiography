Life;Continues

A story of pauses, not endings

The first thing my eyes ever laid on was the face of my mother, struggling to catch her breath, and the gentle face of my father, as if he could not believe I was finally delivered. On the second day of June 2006, inside a hospital in Talobatib, Labo, Camarines Norte, I took my first breath and was introduced to the world as Hanna Jane Poche Betito. That was where the life I continue to write began.

Before anything else, I first learned to memorize and say the names of my parents, Maricel Gerio Poche and Apolinario Rafer Betito. A year after my birth, I was followed by my younger brother, James Poche Betito. We lived in Matanlang, Paracale, Camarines Norte as a complete, simple, and happy family, until I turned seven years old. During those years, I had not yet understood the value of a family or what it meant to be whole. When my father left to work in Saudi Arabia, not a single tear fell from my eyes, only a simple reminder: Dad, don't forget my pasalubong, okay?. As months passed without his presence, the chocolates and money he sent were enough to satisfy my young mind.

Everything changed the moment I saw my mother crying while eating rice with nothing but Magic Sarap to season it with. My mother decided to sell our house and we moved into my grandmother's house in Tawig, Paracale, Camarines Norte, where I once again lived as a child unaware of the weight of reality. Years later, I came to understand that my father had stopped reaching out, and it was only through my grandmother that I learned the truth: that he had left us and built a new family. I did not know what to feel at that time, but I did not cry and I simply continued being a child.

Not long after, my mother followed him to Saudi Arabia to work, driven by the hope of making us whole again. But she did not succeed. Instead, she experienced mistreatment, and after eighteen days, she was sent home by her sibling so she could be with us again. As time passed, the distance between us grew. As my heart drifted away from her, her expectations of me, as the child who would lift her from her failures, grew heavier, until I found myself trapped in the middle of it all.

I was in ninth grade when the pandemic started, and it was then that I experienced hardships no child should have to endure: the emptiness within me, the absence of a father, the longing for my mother's love, and the feeling of having no one to lean on or confide in. I turned to writing as a way to lessen the weight I carried. For a short time, I felt free, but I encountered many different situations which led me to fear and resent the very thing that once became my refuge.

In Grades 11 and 12, I experienced one of the happiest periods of my life. It was when I felt freedom and was able to express my truth. I realized I was part of the LGBTQIA+ community and began to feel attraction to both men and women. I became more open in expressing myself and my thoughts, gained friends, and before my high school life ended, I met the man I would come to love after several attempts at loving before him.

That happiness came to an end when college began. My friends and I went our separate ways, and I started studying at the Polytechnic University of the Philippines. I now stay in a dormitory in Pureza, Sta. Mesa with three of my former high school classmates, including my boyfriend. Living far from what I was used to and keeping up with the fast pace of the city compared to the province was not easy, but I learned to live peacefully with his help.

At present, I am not yet working and do not have a scholarship, which is why I am planning to stop for a while to work and save money. I want to pursue a course that I truly desire, because my heart does not belong to Sociology; I only chose it out of my mother's wishes. Despite this, I continue my studies using my laptop, mobile phone, and WiFi in our dormitory.

During this time, I have developed an interest in programming or computer science through the influence of my boyfriend, and with his help, creating this webpage became possible. At the same time, my desire to write is slowly returning. I love writing poems, whether in English or Filipino, about my life, my struggles, or simply based from my imagination. I want to write freely again; I just need to find my motivation once more.

As my birthday approaches, my world is about to change. As I step into the age of twenty, I will face new challenges. Like what Dory said in Finding Nemo, Just keep swimming, I believe that my story does not end here. No matter how many obstacles and pauses come my way, like a writer who uses a semicolon, I may stop but I will continue moving forward with the flow of life and continuing to write my own story.
